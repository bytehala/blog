What went wrong:

  1. Overcommitting - I said I can finish the app in a week(end). I actually did it in a month because laziness.
  2. Legacy code - I could have done a better job if I just trashed the base code they gave me. Lots of leaks (static Context reference, anyone?) and bad practices on that one. (They had a getter that allowed you to mutate the collection. Migraine!)
  3. Rusty Old Me - Android is evolving really fast, and well, I basically have to strut my stuff to catch up.
  4. Much server calls - The legacy app was designed such that it gets server data every time the user selects a menu item. Could have just mirrored it app-side.
  5. Slow internet - Sometimes I couldn't send the builds on time because the 4G internet wanted to be HSDPA just to piss me off. Google Drive saved me.
  6. Lack of communication - They don't reply as often as I'd like them to.

What went right:

  1. Better - Well, the app turned out better than the legacy app. Did I mention that app was 2014? #Learn2Code.
  2. Redesigned - At least they agreed to let me design the app my way some aspects of the app. The old one was iOS-looking! Eugh!
  3. Practice - I got to brush up on Android and git, which is nice!
  4. They liked it! - The customer said I (we!) was a "star" dev. Their words, not mine. And they want me on board for another project! *Happy dance*

What I learned (Basically Jake Wharton):

  1. Otto! - Otto allowed me to just get on with the coding and not worry about threading shiz.
  2. Animations - (Actually I didn't? LOL. Patrick did this part, but I read up on it after the "release*".)
  3. OkHttp - Took much REST headache out of my hands.
  4. Memory Analyzer Tool - I've used this before, but now I can use it with Android Studio.
  5. Google Stuff - Maps, Analytics, AdView
  6. Android Studio - We used AS for the project, so I learned some commonly used shortcuts and how to not be intimidated by Gradle/Project Settings.
  7. Schedule - To keep your customer informed, you have to have a regular schedule for builds. A daily one worked for me, even though I don't think they tested every one I sent.
